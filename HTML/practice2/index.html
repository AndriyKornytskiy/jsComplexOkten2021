Reebok

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

For the South African antelope, see Grey rhebok.

Reebok International Limited (/ˈriːbɒk/) is an Anglo-American footwear and clothing company that has been a subsidiary[4] of German sporting goods giant Adidas since August 2005.[5][6][7] Reebok produces and distributes fitness, running and CrossFit sportswear including clothing and footwear. It is the official footwear and apparel sponsor for Ultimate Fighting Championship (UFC), CrossFit, and Spartan Race.[8]

In 1958, Reebok was established as a companion company to J.W. Foster and Sons, founded in 1895 in Bolton, Lancashire, England.[9] From 1958 until 1986, Reebok clothing featured the Union Flag to signify the company's British origins.[10]

The company's global headquarters are located in Boston, Massachusetts,[11] U.S., with regional offices in Amsterdam, Montreal, Hong Kong, and Mexico City.

In November 2016, Reebok announced it would be moving its headquarters from the Boston suburb of Canton to the innovation and design building in the seaport district of South Boston. The reasons for the move, according to the company, were to be located in an urban environment that is more desirable to millennial workers and to “clarify the roles” of United States offices.[12] The move was completed in fall of 2018.[13]

History

Early history

In 1895, Joseph William Foster at the age of 14 started work in his bedroom above his father's sweatshop in Bolton, England, and designed some of the earliest spiked running shoes.[14] After his ideas progressed, he founded his business 'J.W. Foster' in 1900, later he joined with his sons and changed the company name to J.W. Foster and Sons.[15][16] Foster opened a small factory called Olympic Works, and gradually became famous among athletes for his "running pumps".[14] For pioneering the use of spikes, the company's revolutionary running pumps appear in the book, Golden Kicks: The Shoes that changed Sport.[17] The company began distributing shoes across the Union Jack flag which were worn by British athletes.[17] They were made famous by 100m Olympic champion Harold Abrahams (who would be immortalised in the Oscar winning film Chariots of Fire) in the 1924 Summer Olympics held in Paris.[17][18]

In 1958, in Bolton, two of the founder's grandsons, Joe and Jeff Foster, formed a companion company "Reebok," having found the name in a South African dictionary won in a running race by Joe Foster as a boy.[15] The name is Afrikaans for the grey rhebok, a type of African antelope.[17][19]

U.S. market

In 1979, at the Chicago International Sneaker Trade show an American businessman, Paul Fireman, took notice of Reebok. Fireman was working for team sports and negotiated a deal to license and distribute the Reebok brand in the United States. The division was called Reebok USA Ltd.[20] Later that year, Fireman introduced three new shoes to the market at $60. By 1981, Reebok reached more than $1.5 million in sales.[9]

1980s–1990s

In 1982, Reebok debuted the Reebok Freestyle aerobics shoe, the first athletic shoe designed for women.[9][21] The following year, Reebok's sales were $13 million.[22]

The company began expanding from tennis and aerobics shoes to running and basketball throughout the mid to late 1980s, the largest segments of the athletic footwear industry at the time.[23] Fireman bought the English-based parent company in 1984. In addition to its IPO, the brand will conquer[promotion?] tennis with the Newport Classic, popularized by Boris Becker and John Mc Enroe, and the Revenge Plus, better known today under its name Reebok Club C.

Officially an American company in 1985, Reebok had its initial public offering on the New York Stock Exchange under the ticker symbol RBK.[18][23]

In 1986, Reebok switched its logo from the Union Jack flag it had since its founding, to the Vector logo - an abstract Union Jack streak across a race track - which mirrored the design of the side flashes of its shoes[24][25] The switch signaled the transition of the company into a performance brand as it began licensing deals with professional athletes in the NBA and NFL.[26]

During the 1980s, Reebok began introducing sports clothing and accessories (alongside entering the college/pro sports arena), along with a new line of children's athletic shoes (called 'Weeboks').[27] By mid-decade, Reebok's sales were about $1 billion.[6] One of the company's most iconic technologies,[28] the Reebok Pump, debuted in 1989[29][30] with more than 100 professional athletes wearing the footwear by 1992, including Shaquille O'Neal.[9][31]

Reebok worked with fitness professional Gin Miller in the late 1980s to develop Step Reebok, based on Miller's wooden prototype step and her ideas for step aerobics. The Step was evaluated in physiology trials undertaken by Drs. Lorna and Peter Francis at San Diego State University. In August 1989 the Step was ready, made in molded plastic by Sports Step of Atlanta with Reebok's name on it,[32] and by March 1990, the step aerobics classes were attracting media attention.[33] Miller promoted Step Reebok in person, touring the U.S. and demonstrating it at exercise studios. Step aerobics became widely popular, helping the company sell many thousands of adjustable-height step devices, and millions of high-top shoes with ankle support.[19] Step aerobics peaked in 1995 with 11.4 million people exercising in that style.[34]

Reebok named Carl Yankowski president and chief executive officer of the brand in 1998, replacing former president Robert Meers.[35][36] Yankowski stepped down one year later to accept an executive position at another company. Reebok chairman and CEO Paul Fireman took over as president for the first time in 12 years.[36]
